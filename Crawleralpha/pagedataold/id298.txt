Story: Yudi loves girls but hates commitment - what happens when he meets commitment-averse Aanchal? Review: Happy Ending stylishly remixes Saif Ali Khan's trademark avatar as a brat who won't grow up. Saif plays Yudi, author in LA, high on sex and success. Yudi avoids new work and commitment - which his ex (Preity Zinta in cameo, channeling the pair's Salaam Namaste chemistry) underlines. His married buddy Montu (Ranvir Shorey) is Yudi's party partner while his inner voice is a chubby Yogi (Saif) warning him not to ring his 'axe'. Suddenly, Yudi's money runs out. His publishers switch to hot-shot novelist Aanchal (Ileana) who brushes off Yudi's charms. Pushy Bollywood superstar Armaan (Govinda) demands Yudi write a DVD-inspired rom-com while dentist Vishakha (Kalki) pursues him obsessively. How does Yudi complete this tale? movie Happy Ending has happy ingredients like sparkling dialogues, certain lines - "Tumhari reading pe kuch log so gaye - kuch mar bhi gaye the" - laugh-out-loud hilarious. Key performances stand out. Saif stylishly pulls off his charmer plus bummer inner voice, fat, hairy, spouting philosophy. Laughing at himself, Saif's terrific when he mumbles at Aanchal, "Mageoudwivme?" The other scene-stealer is Govinda as Armaan-ji, a sharply written character, seeking tailor-made abs plus a story with "comedy, chumma-chaati and kick-aas". Ranvir Shorey displays deadpan ease while Kalki's uninhibited as sweetly suffocating Vishakha. Ileana's breezy as Aanchal who isn't seeking love - but loves the occasional romp. However, the film is about 30 minutes too long. A rom-com should stay feather-light but multiple cars and stars stretch this one. The editing sometimes flags, making the plot feel like a string of sit-com situations, while the lead pair's chemistry eventually wanes, the ending a tad tame. But mostly, Happy Ending's a fun film with a melodious, modern take on individuals and pyaar. It's an NRI movie that side-steps dilemmas and pain. And it's probably the first Hindi film where the heroine cheekily tells the hero, "Show your boobs!", performed with polished elan. Wrapping Hollywood ribbons on a Bollywood heart, this is a nice ending - and a good start. In-depth Analysis Our overall critic’s rating is not an average of the sub scores below. Direction 3.5/5 Dialogues 4.0/5 Story 3.5/5 Music 3.5/5 Visual appeal 3.5/5 PAGE 1PAGE 2PAGE 3PAGE 4 Film: Happy Ending Cast: Saif Ali Khan, Ileana D'Cruz, Govinda, Kalki Koechlin, Ranvir Shorey, Preity Zinta Directors: Raj Nidimoru, Krishna DK Certification: U/A Rating: A profound moment in Spike Jonze's Her has Joaquin Phoenix wondering aloud, "Sometimes I think I've felt everything I'm ever gonna feel. From here on, I'm not gonna feel anything new—just lesser versions of what I've already felt." This line echoes my sentiments about urban romantic comedies. Perhaps even directors Raj and Krishna DK are aware too; that this genre has nothing new to offer anymore. Seen before, done before. Narrative styles, conflicts, background scores and supporting characters have begun to overlap. All that differ are the littler details—professions, environments, guest appearances. This explains why, within these constraints, Happy Ending visibly strains to be self-aware and outsmart the genre. I'm a sucker for films about writers, even if they're filthy rich one-book wonders living in LA for no particular reason. Yudi (Saif) is that miracle here, with crude alter ego Yogi (resembling Pritam). For quick cash, he agrees to write an 'international' screenplay for a single-screen superstar (Govinda). This sets the tone for the old meta film-within-a-film template which, for portions of a breezy first half, partially succeeds. Of course, he is a playboy too. Therefore, as per convention, he breaks the fourth wall to spout frothy fundas, his married friend (Shorey; in form) serves as 'Shaadi Ke Side Effects' relief, his metropolitan wit and pokerface counter over-attached girlfriends (Kalki; scene-stealer), rave, repeat. Everyone's in their comfort zones, and some funny lines pull towards an interesting trigger—Govinda. He crackles in his few scenes, nailing the sly classes v/s masses take with gems like, "For 300 bucks (ticket), don't try teaching the audience how to live." His introduction presents an opportunity to turn the genre on its head. Yudi is automatically forced to let his (love)life dictate the progress of his banal project: A perfect setup to construct an unconventional narrative, while deconstructing every cliche in the book. But an ex-flame (Zinta) advises Yudi to stop living life like a comedy and start living it like a love story. She shouldn't have. Because there's an exact moment when this happens—leading to a contrived road-trip that serves as an extended excuse to showcase America's coastline. The film nosedives, and switches into a meandering tale of self-discovery. This is unfortunate for Ileana D'Cruz who, as his love interest and younger writing sensation, looks comfortable even as the script falls prey to the same formulaic holes it pokes fun at. Perhaps the intention was to demonstrate that real life is full of cliches too, and try hard as one may, they cannot be escaped. But pre-stamping traditional sequences with unimaginative plot headers (Love, Re-entry, Climax) does not make the graph less dreary to watch. Saif Ali Khan possesses a solo-hero aura that is often at odds with Indian locales, and gait that seamlessly merges into First World photography. He carries on from Kal Ho Naa Ho, Salaam Namaste and Cocktail to partly demolish memories of Humshakals. Average fare, but an extra half-star for inventive dialogues; for reminding us that his timing is intact, if only just. However, there's no denying that he has seen better days, as have the filmmakers. They're known for reinvention (99, Shor In The City) and innovation (Go Goa Gone), but with Happy Ending, they've played it safe. Way too Saif. PLOT SUMMARY Writer Yudi (Saif Ali Khan) wrote a hit book five and a half years ago but he's still living life king-size in LA, enjoying fame and affairs, avoiding commitment, doing no work, partying with his miserably married friend Montu (Ranvir Shorey) or having conversations with his fat, bearded inner voice Yogi (also Saif). Suddenly, Yudi's money runs out and his publishers dump him for hit romance novelist Aanchal (Ileana D'Cruz), visiting from Mumbai. The only work Yudi now gets is from Bollywood single-screen superstar Armaan (Govinda) who insists he copies Hollywood movies and writes a multiplex-style rom-com. Meanwhile, Yudi's dentist and once-girlfriend Vishakha (Kalki) is pursuing him obsessively, even loading videos of him singing in the shower on her laptop, forcing Yudi and Montu to break into her home when she's away. On top of this, Yudi's got severe Writer's Block, stopping him from finishing any work. Running away from it all, Yudi tries to charm Aanchal but she's commitment-averse too. However, she gradually warms upto him, escorting him to Armaan's party, trying to help Yudi write, even agreeing to take a road trip with him to her book reading in San Francisco. The duo has fun singing classic Bollywood numbers on the way until doziness overtakes them and their car skids. While they wait for the repair van, Yudi and Aanchal have a ball posing sexily on the highway in search of lifts. Eventually reaching San Francisco, the couple's forced to share a hotel room - and agreeing to keep it simple and commitment-free, they end up making love. Their relationship grows funnier - on Aanchal's last night in LA, they argue over dinner, Yudi throws her phone into a vase while she douses him with water - but neither expresses anything more than a sense of light fun. It's only when Yudi's ex Divya (Priety Zinta in a cameo), having argued with her husband and visiting Yudi with her kids, tells him he loves Aanchal that he admits it to himself. Yudi then races Aanchal's taxi to the airport in a hilarious scene and tells her his feelings - but she says, thanks, let's just be friends. As she leaves, Yudi's sunk in depression - and then starts to write. Along the way, Vishakha gets engaged to a panic-struck Yudi by falsely claiming she's pregnant - but then breaks it up, saying she doesn't want him to marry her out of pity. Armaan, who's meanwhile got himself cosmetic surgery and six-pack abs in LA, is impressed with the 'kick-ass' script Yudi presents him. However, for the script's ending, Yudi goes to Mumbai and sees Aanchal where he tells her they can take it one day at a time - and perhaps live their whole lives together like that.
